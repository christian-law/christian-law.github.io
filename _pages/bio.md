I am a PhD candidate in Criminology and Justice Policy at Northeastern University and
an incoming Assistant Professor of Sociology and Criminology at Butler University.

I study how intersecting systems of inequality — rooted in race, gender, and class — 
shape the way Americans think about crime, punishment, and justice. Put simply, I explore 
to understand how perceptions of threat to racial and gender privilege drive public opinion 
on punishment, protests, and social policy.

My path to this work began at California Lutheran University, where I graduated 
with a B.S. in Criminology and Criminal Justice. It was there, as a Ronald E. 
McNair Scholar, that I designed and fielded my own first survey exploring 
how college students viewed race and policing at the **height** of the 2020 Black Lives 
Matter protests. 

At Northeastern, I am now completing my dissertation, which explores how Americans navigate a 
contested terrain of racialized crime politics in an era of rising antiracism and 
resurgent racial appeals. Across three interrelated studies, I examine trends in how 
Americans think about race and crime, and how racial crime framing evokes crime policy and 
police reform preferences.

Beyond the dissertation, my broader research agenda asks how racial and social 
hierarchies are maintained — and contested — through crime and punishment. That 
question keeps pulling me toward the intersections of politics, public opinion, and 
justice: from the policing of Black Lives Matter protests, to attacks against education
on race and racism, to the prosecution of January 6th insurrectionists.

I've had some great teachers and mentors throughout my life. They've shown me not only how to 
be a critical scholar, but also how to be a better person.
My goal in the classroom is to help students develop critical knowledge about systems of oppression, **and** 
embrace a humanist lens and advocacy mindset to do something about them.
